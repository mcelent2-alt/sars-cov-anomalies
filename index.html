01 — Nomenclature Anomaly

The Naming Problem

The name "SARS-CoV-1" was only applied to the original 2003 virus after the COVID-19 pandemic began. Prior to 2020, it was simply known as SARS-CoV. Yet five pre-2019 papers used the sequential virus-lineage format CoV1/CoV2/CoV3 — precisely the format ICTV would later officially adopt.

```
🔍 Key Finding

No pre-2019 SARS-CoV paper uses "SARS-CoV2" or "SARS-CoV3"… except for 5.

These papers — from 2006, 2008, and 2015 — are the only instances of this exact nomenclature format in all pre-pandemic scientific literature.

Critically, the 2021 WHO working group explicitly rejected sequential numbering because it confuses sequence databases — yet these papers used it in sequence-database-dependent work (GenBank citations, RT-PCR calibration) without any peer reviewer flagging it.

❓ What Makes It Unusual
  • → Format precision: "SARS-CoV1 / SARS-CoV2 / SARS-CoV3" is exactly the virus-lineage format ICTV later adopted — not a fragment label.
  • → Standard alternatives existed: Normal practice would label fragments as "SARS-CoV fragment 1/2/3," "amplicon A/B/C," or "region 1/2/3."
  • → Uniqueness: No other pre-2019 paper prefixes multiple coronavirus fragments from the same isolate this way.
  • → Sustained silence: Authors continued publishing SARS-CoV-2 work (2020–2025) without a single clarifying footnote.
"The 2021 WHO working group explicitly rejected sequential numbering because it creates confusion with genetic sequence databases — the very context these papers operate in (GenBank citations, primer sets, RT-PCR calibration)."
— From the research document by Matthew Celentano
Standard Alternatives That Were NOT Chosen
✗ "SARS-CoV fragment 1/2/3"
✗ "SARS-CoV amplicon A/B/C"
✗ "SARS-CoV region 1/2/3"
✗ "Fragment 1/2/3" (standard chimeric)

The virus-lineage format SARS-CoV1 / SARS-CoV2 / SARS-CoV3 was chosen instead.
```

The AY864806 Evidence

```
The Central Point: In virology, these regions are routinely referred to by functional names — RdRp fragment, helicase fragment, N-gene fragment. The authors instead chose the virus-implying sequential labels SARS-CoV1/2/3. The anomaly is not the sequences themselves (which are clearly from 2003), but the labeling choice, its uniqueness in the literature, the institutional silence since 2020, and the precise match to future official nomenclature.
```

04 — Skeptical Analysis

Arguments & Counters

The document rigorously presents both sides. Below are the seven main skeptical arguments and their rebuttals. Click each to expand.

```
Argument

The parentheses group SARS-CoV1, CoV2, CoV3 within one entity, making clear these are fragments of a single virus, not separate lineages.

Counter

The parentheses group them — but still use the exact virus-lineage format ICTV later adopted. If it was neutral shorthand, the sentence would read: "SARS-CoV fragments 1, 2, and 3" or "SARS-CoV amplicons A/B/C." Neither was chosen.

Argument

Sequential numbering is common in diagnostics. With one accession number cited, any reader would understand these aren't separate viruses.

Counter

Zero other pre-2019 papers prefix coronavirus fragments from one isolate as "SARS-CoV1/2/3." The 2021 WHO working group explicitly banned sequential numbering because it confuses sequence databases — the exact context these papers operate in.

Argument

The WHO rejection was about GISAID lineage naming in the context of 2021, not diagnostic calibrator labels from 2008.

Counter

WHO rejected it because it confuses sequence databases. These papers used the same format in sequence-database-dependent work: GenBank AY864806, BNI primer sets, RT-PCR calibration. The context is identical. The rejection proves the format was not harmless shorthand.

Argument

Niche methodological papers in diagnostic chemistry don't attract virology scrutiny or require errata for minor naming conventions.

Counter

Post-2020, the 2006 paper reached an Altmetric score of 1,209 — ranked #2 in all Clinical Chemistry. Qingge Li published SARS-CoV-2 diagnostics through 2022–2025 using this exact platform. One footnote would resolve it. He never added one.

Argument

Researchers may have used sequential Mandarin numbering which translated awkwardly into the English SARS-CoV1/2/3 format.

Counter

The 2006 paper shows sophisticated English and precise technical terminology throughout. Authors published in high-impact English journals. Translation issues would manifest more broadly — not only in this specific nomenclature choice that precisely matches future official designations.

Argument

When handling multiple related sequences, numbering them is natural. The match with future nomenclature reflects shared logical frameworks, not foreknowledge.

Counter

If this format was truly intuitive, we would find it in many other coronavirus calibrator papers from the same era. We find zero. The format appears in essentially no other pre-2019 coronavirus papers despite the same logical motivation existing universally.

Argument

Researchers don't typically respond to every internet theory about their old papers, especially from non-experts.

Counter

Li is actively publishing SARS-CoV-2 diagnostics through 2022–2025 using the same armored RNA platform he pioneered in 2006. The anomaly is embedded in his foundational paper. During a global pandemic with the paper reaching an Altmetric score of 1,209, one footnote would end the debate. He has never added it.

Overall Assessment
Strongest Skeptical Points
  • •Translation artifacts could explain some anomalies
  • •Retrospective pattern recognition may amplify significance
  • •Conference circuit information transfer remains possible
Points Skeptics Cannot Explain
  • •Sustained institutional silence over 6+ years of pandemic attention
  • •Statistical clustering of anomalies around this research group
  • •Peer review at high-quality journals without flagging
  • •WHO's own explicit rejection of this exact format for these exact reasons
```

```
Jinming Li & China's 2020–21 EQA Program

Jinming Li — corresponding author of both 2008 papers and the patent — ran China's official nationwide External Quality Assessment (EQA) program for every SARS-CoV-2 RT-PCR test in the country in 2020–21, distributing armored RNA calibrators built on the exact 2008 patented platform to 844 laboratories.

B

Xiamen University ↔ McLellan Collaboration

Ningshao Xia (Director of the National Institute of Diagnostics at Xiamen University — the same institution as Qingge Li's lab) co-authored RSV protein stabilization papers with Jason McLellan in 2013. McLellan's lab later developed the 2P stabilized spike protein foundational to Moderna, Pfizer-BioNTech, and J&J COVID-19 vaccines. Nianshuang Wang, who co-developed the 2P mutation in McLellan's lab, moved to UT Austin shortly after McLellan did in 2018.

2013 RSV Paper — PubMed 23618766 ↗

Penn Medicine "COVID-19 Research" — March 13, 2019

The University of Pennsylvania Perelman School of Medicine's iPSC Core website maintained a "Research Spotlights" entry dated Wednesday, March 13, 2019: "…the IPS Core is developing models to study the effects of SARS-COV-2 in human pluripotent stem cells." This predates the official naming of the virus by nearly a year.

Penn Medicine iPSC Core ↗

DTRA Ukraine Sub-Award: "COVID-19 Research" — November 12, 2019

Under DTRA parent contract HDTRA108D0007 (Black & Veatch Ukraine Biological Threat Reduction Program, ~$116M), a $369,511 sub-award to Labyrinth Global Health Inc. was issued November 12, 2019 for "SME Manuscript Documentation and COVID-19 Research." This was three months before the WHO declared a public health emergency.

World Bank WITS Trade Data Labeled "COVID-19 Test Kits" for 2017–2018

WITS (World Bank) trade database showed imports/exports labeled "COVID-19 Test kits" for years 2017 and 2018 (HS codes 300215, 382200, 902780). Moderna Patent Sequence — 19-Nucleotide Match
```

06 — Implications

Open Questions

The document does not make definitive claims. It identifies patterns, documents primary evidence, and poses questions that remain unaddressed by institutions with the resources to answer them.

```
?

Why use the virus-lineage format at all?

If the SARS-CoV1/2/3 labels were merely convenient shorthand for gene fragments, why choose the one format that implies separate viral lineages — the same format ICTV later adopted officially — over universally more common alternatives like "fragment A/B/C" or "region 1/2/3"?

?

Why no clarification from the authors?

Qingge Li and Jinming Li are both active researchers who have published extensively on SARS-CoV-2 diagnostics since 2020 using the exact platforms described in these papers. Neither has added a single footnote, comment, or erratum clarifying the 2006/2008 nomenclature — despite the 2006 paper reaching global attention with an Altmetric score of 1,209.

?

How was Jinming Li's lab positioned so perfectly for 2020?

Li's group had pre-existing MMCA technology for mutation detection, established R/Shiny infrastructure for rapid web tool deployment, and deep institutional expertise in armored RNA calibrators for exactly the type of virus they would need to test. The transition from genome release (January 10, 2020) to operational nationwide diagnostic infrastructure within weeks is either extraordinary foresight or pre-existing preparation.

?

Why did COVID-19 origin investigations miss this?

While Ralph Baric faced years of congressional hearings, FOIA requests, and public scrutiny, and while numerous Chinese labs received intense investigation, the Xiamen University / Beijing Hospital research group — which exhibited prescient nomenclature 14 years early and had immediate 2020 deployment capabilities — appears to have received no investigative attention from oversight bodies in the US or elsewhere.

From the author
```
